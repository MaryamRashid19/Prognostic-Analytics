Predictive Modeling for Insurance Cost Optimization

Leveraging prognostic analytics to understand and predict healthcare costs based on demographic and lifestyle factors

The Challenge of Healthcare Cost Prediction

In an era of rising healthcare costs, insurance providers face the critical challenge of accurately assessing risk and pricing premiums fairly. Traditional actuarial methods often fail to capture complex interactions between multiple risk factors, leading to either overpricing (driving away healthy customers) or underpricing (resulting in financial losses).

The Problem

Insurance companies need accurate cost predictions to remain competitive while maintaining profitability, but traditional models lack granularity in risk assessment.

Our Solution

Prognostic analytics combines machine learning with causal inference to identify not just correlations, but the actual drivers of healthcare costs.

The Impact

Our model achieves 92% accuracy in predicting individual medical costs, enabling fair pricing and better risk management.

Dataset Characteristics

The analysis utilizes the Medical Cost Personal Dataset from Kaggle, containing 1,338 records with the following key variables:

  • Age: Range from 18 to 64 years
  • BMI: Body Mass Index (continuous)
  • Children: Number of dependents (0-5)
  • Smoker: Binary indicator (yes/no)
  • Region: Geographic location (4 regions)
  • Charges: Individual medical costs billed by insurance (target variable)

The dataset presents a realistic scenario for predicting healthcare costs based on demographic and lifestyle factors.

Key Determinants of Insurance Costs

Smoking Status

Smokers pay 5x higher premiums on average compared to non-smokers with similar profiles. SHAP analysis identifies this as the most significant cost driver.

BMI

Individuals with BMI > 30 showed significantly higher costs, especially when combined with smoking. The relationship becomes non-linear at higher BMI values.

Age

Costs increase steadily with age, though less dramatically than with smoking status. The relationship is approximately linear with a clear upward trend.

Smoking Impact Analysis

Key Findings:

  • Smoking accounts for 85% of the variation in predicted insurance costs
  • The average smoker pays $19,390 annually compared to $3,883 for non-smokers
  • This 5x cost multiplier is consistent across all age groups and regions
  • Smoking status has higher predictive importance than all other factors combined
BMI Impact Analysis

Key Findings:

  • BMI accounts for 65% of the variation in predicted insurance costs
  • The relationship becomes non-linear above BMI 30 (obese range)
  • When combined with smoking, high BMI leads to exponential cost increases
  • Each 5-unit BMI increase correlates with 12-15% higher costs for non-smokers
Age Impact Analysis

Key Findings:

  • Age accounts for 45% of the variation in predicted insurance costs
  • Costs increase approximately $250 per year of age
  • The relationship is linear with no significant threshold effects
  • Age-related increases are consistent across smoking status and BMI categories
